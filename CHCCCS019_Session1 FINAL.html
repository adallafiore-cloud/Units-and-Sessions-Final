Content Block 1: Defining a Crisis

10:15 – 10:30 · Learner Guide pp. 5–6
📖 Chapter 1.1 · pp. 5–6

What exactly makes something a "crisis"?

The word gets used loosely — but in community services, it has a specific meaning that shapes how you respond. Let's build a shared definition.

🔬 Research & Discuss (5 min) — Slide 7

Before we look at the Learner Guide definition: what do you think a crisis is? In pairs, come up with your own definition and two examples of events that might trigger a crisis. Then share with the class.

In community services, a crisis is generally understood as a state in which the person is unable to function in their usual way or by using their accustomed resources, often due to a significantly stressful event. Crisis events cause emotional, psychological and physical distress and affect the person's behaviour and ability to function.

The American psychiatrist Gerard Caplan defines a crisis as occurring when a person faces an obstacle to important life goals that is, for a time, insurmountable by customary means of problem solving.

Turning PointTap to reveal
A crisis marks a turning point — a moment where things must change. It presents both risk and the possibility of meaningful growth.
Unable to CopeTap to reveal
The person cannot solve the problem using their usual strategies. Their normal coping resources — practical, emotional, social — are overwhelmed.
Crisis InterventionTap to reveal
Short-term, immediate help aimed at reducing distress and helping the person access resources to resolve the crisis. Think of it as first aid for mental health.
Triggering EventTap to reveal
The event or situation the person finds overwhelming and cannot deal with alone. Can be major (disaster) or seemingly minor (a failed exam, a breakup).

Triggering events fall into three broad categories:

Natural disasters, health and wellbeing events, violence, financial hardship and other high-risk situations. These are often sudden and external — things that happen to us.
The breakdown of relationships and abusive relationships. Relationship loss or rupture is one of the most common triggers for crisis across the lifespan.
Issues relating to developmental stages — adolescence, mid-life transitions, ageing, changes in social roles and status. These are predictable but can still be overwhelming when they arrive.
💡

Why this matters in practice

Understanding that a crisis can be triggered by an "ordinary" event — failing a driving test, a friend moving away, a job rejection — stops workers from minimising or dismissing what a client is experiencing. The crisis is in the person's experience of the event, not just the event itself.

As a class: What are some events in everyday life — things that might seem small from the outside — that could trigger a genuine crisis for some people? What factors might make someone more vulnerable to crisis in that moment?

⚡ Activity 1 · True or False — Defining Crisis

Crisis: True or False?

Read each statement and select True or False. Feedback appears instantly — these connect directly to what you just read.

1. A crisis must be triggered by a catastrophic external event to be a genuine crisis.

False. Even everyday events like a breakup, a job loss, or failing an exam can trigger a genuine crisis. The key is that the person cannot function in their usual way or use their accustomed coping resources.

2. Crisis intervention is primarily focused on long-term therapeutic change.

False. Crisis intervention consists of short-term, immediate help. Its aim is to reduce distress so the person can identify and use their own resources. Long-term support is a separate step.

3. A crisis can present both threats AND opportunities for personal growth.

True. This is a key idea in crisis theory. A crisis is a turning point — it is dangerous, but it also creates conditions for change and growth that might not otherwise occur.

4. Triggering events can include relationship breakdowns and developmental life transitions like adolescence or mid-life.

True. The Learner Guide identifies three categories of triggering events: situations, relationships, and life changes/developmental issues. All three are valid crisis triggers.

Content Block 2: Signs, Indicators & Communication in Crisis

10:45 – 12:00 · Learner Guide pp. 7–8
📖 Chapter 1.2 · pp. 7–8

How do you recognise when someone is in crisis?

Recognition is the first step — and it is not always obvious. This section builds your observation toolkit.

To provide appropriate support, you need to identify signs that a person is experiencing a crisis, or that a crisis is imminent — about to happen. Signs fall into two categories:

👁️

Direct Indicators

Observable signs that something has happened to cause significant distress — visible, identifiable, immediately apparent to a trained observer.

🔍

Indirect Indicators

Less obvious — behaviours and verbal statements that hint at distress without directly naming it. Changes in mood, social engagement, physical health, substance use.

Direct indicators include:

1
A triggering event has occurred ▼
There is a clear disaster situation, serious health event, relationship breakdown, or significant life change that can be identified as the starting point of the crisis.
2
Distorted perceptions of the event ▼
The person's interpretation of what has happened becomes distorted — they may catastrophise, misread intentions, or believe consequences that are unlikely or untrue.
3
Loss of problem-solving ability ▼
The person can no longer respond appropriately to the event. Their usual strategies for managing stress or difficulty no longer work in this situation.
4
Emotions overwhelm functioning ▼
The person's emotional response stops them from functioning effectively. Fear, panic, dread, and despair can take over their capacity to act or think clearly.
📖 Case Study · Learner Guide p. 7

John and Mary — After the Bushfire

John and Mary have recently lost their house and all possessions in a bushfire. They are now staying with Mary's parents with their three children.

John is experiencing panic attacks and cannot think clearly. He believes his wife will divorce him and he will never see his children again. Each morning he returns to the ruins of his house, searching fruitlessly through rubble. He has stopped eating, cannot sleep, avoids friends and family, sits alone drinking at night, and has become short-tempered with Mary and the children.

🔍 Discuss — Slide 14

Looking at John's situation: identify at least four signs of crisis — both direct and indirect. For each sign, name whether it is direct or indirect and explain what it tells you about John's functioning. You don't need to share personal stories — professional examples work perfectly.

Communication in crisis is also affected. People revert to fight-or-flight reasoning, which changes how they process information:

Important information gets missed or misinterpreted
People fall back on existing beliefs, struggle to absorb new information
They fill gaps with speculation when information is scarce
Emotions: fear, anxiety, dread, despair, powerlessness
Denial is common — hard to believe the threat is real
Panic can result from extreme fight-or-flight stress response
💬

As a practitioner

Knowing how communication shifts in crisis means you adjust your approach: slow down, use simple language, repeat key information, check understanding, and do not assume that because someone nodded they have actually absorbed what you said.

Content Block 3: Types of Crisis Situations & Safety Issues

12:45 – 1:45 · Learner Guide pp. 9–20
⚠️ Sensitive Content · Chapter 1.3–1.4 · pp. 9–20

The situations you will actually face in community services work.

This section covers suicide, self-harm, threats, abuse, and domestic and family violence. These are real, frequent, and serious. You don't need to share personal stories — professional examples work perfectly. If you need a moment, take it.

There are several different types of crisis situations you may encounter in your work. They may arise as a response to a triggering event, or they may themselves be the triggering event that leads to further crisis.

⚠️ Crisis Type: Potential Suicide

Responding to Potential Suicide

Your priority must always be to keep the person safe. Work within your organisation's policies and procedures. The three steps:

A
ASK — Ask them directly ▼
Ask directly: "Are you thinking about suicide?" Do not be afraid — it shows you care. Asking directly actually decreases risk because it opens the conversation. Make sure you ask unambiguously.
L
LISTEN & STAY — Do not leave them alone ▼
If they say yes, listen and allow them to express how they are feeling. Do not leave them alone. Stay with them or arrange for a reliable person to stay.
G
GET HELP — Connect to appropriate support ▼
Call Lifeline 13 11 14 or 000 if life is in immediate danger. Offer to make GP or psychologist appointments and accompany the person if they wish. Even when danger is not immediate, they will likely need longer-term support.

She continues to attend her ballet classes and visit her grandmother Lisa at the nursing home every Sunday — activities that suggest protective factors are still present.

You may encounter threats of harm in contexts of domestic and family violence, child protection, mental health, drug and alcohol work, or residential services. Your first concern is to keep everyone as safe as possible.

Steps: Identify who is at risk and how serious the risks are. Use observation skills and ask questions. If the threat is imminent, remove yourself and others from danger if possible, then call for help. Always follow your organisation's policies and procedures.

Self-harm is any behaviour involving the deliberate causing of pain or injury to oneself — cutting, burning, biting, scratching, hair-pulling, self-hitting, placing oneself in danger, or abuse of drugs/alcohol including overdose.

Responding: If necessary, provide first aid and call for help. Use active listening. Remain calm and non-judgemental. Encourage the person to seek professional help from someone they trust.

Threatening, aggressive, and violent behaviour often stems from fear, anger, and defensiveness. There is no simple blueprint for responding, but keeping everyone as safe as possible is always the priority. Follow your organisation's policies and procedures — and use common sense.

Abuse is any action or behaviour that causes intentional harm — verbal, emotional, psychological, financial, economic, or physical. In abuse situations, there is typically an imbalance of power where the person being abused is more dependent on or has less power than the abuser.

Service approaches include rights-based, person-centred, and strengths-based frameworks — all focused on empowerment, advocacy, and individual needs.

Domestic violence refers to violent behaviour between current or former intimate partners — one partner exerts power and control over the other, usually through fear. Can include physical, sexual, emotional, social, verbal, spiritual, and economic abuse.

Family violence is a broader term covering violence between family members including siblings and parent-child relationships. It is the preferred term when working with Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities, acknowledging extended family and kinship structures.

James and Sam — Responding to Self-Harm

James is a physical education teacher who notices fresh cuts on the arms of his student Sam after a football match. He takes Sam aside, sits next to him, and asks how he got the cuts. Sam bursts into tears and says he often cuts himself when he feels "down."

James talks quietly to Sam, asks about times he feels down, reassures him, and explains that people sometimes self-harm as a way of coping with painful feelings. He offers to refer Sam to a service for young people and to accompany him to see a counsellor.

1.4 Safety Issues — Learner Guide pp. 18–19

In responding to any crisis situation, safety is always the primary consideration. You must be able to recognise and respond to direct and indirect indications that safety is at risk.

1
Listen empathetically to collect information ▼
Use active listening skills — not just hearing words, but reflecting feelings and building trust so the person feels safe enough to share what is actually happening.
2
Affirm the person's links to safety and living ▼
Identify and name protective factors — the people, activities, values, and commitments that connect the person to life. These are the foundations of a safety plan.
3
Support the person to identify solutions ▼
Don't impose solutions — help the person identify their own possible strategies. Use collaborative and directive approaches depending on the person's current capacity.
4
Work with the person to agree on actions to minimise risk ▼
Collaboratively agree on specific steps. These form the core of a safety plan — they need to be realistic and achievable within the person's actual circumstances and resources.
5
Call for emergency assistance if required ▼
Do not wait until all other steps are complete. If immediate danger is present, call 000. Emergency assistance can be called at any point — safety trumps process.
6
Check your actions are legal, ethical, and meet duty of care ▼
Before, during, and after: verify your actions comply with your organisation's policies, relevant legislation, and the ethical frameworks of your profession.

SMART goals are the foundation of effective safety planning. Safety plans need goals that are:

🎯

Specific

Everyone knows exactly what should be done — no ambiguity about the action required.

📏

Measurable

Clear and understandable so everyone knows when the goal has been achieved.

✅

Achievable

Able to be accomplished within a reasonable time frame with the resources available.

🤝

Relevant

The person has input and agreement — goals that matter to them and fit their situation.

⏰

Timely

Clear time frames are set — when will this happen? By when do we review?
