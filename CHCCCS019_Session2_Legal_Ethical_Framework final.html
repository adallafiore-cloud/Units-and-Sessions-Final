Duty of Care — What You're Actually Obligated to Do

10:15–10:30 · Learner Guide pp. 22–28

⚖️ Chapter 2 · Section 2.1

What does "reasonable" actually mean — and how does it protect you?

Duty of care is a concept many people think they understand until a real situation tests it.

People in crisis are especially vulnerable. Duty of care is a legal principle based on common law requiring everyone to avoid causing reasonably foreseeable harm to others through careless behaviour. You're not expected to predict rare events, but you are expected to use common sense.

Duty of CareTap to reveal
A legal obligation to avoid causing reasonably foreseeable harm through careless action or inaction
NegligenceTap to reveal
Carelessness that causes harm — the basis for civil legal action when duty of care is breached
Reasonably ForeseeableTap to reveal
What a reasonable person could predict might happen — not rare or extreme events you couldn't have anticipated
Standard of CareTap to reveal
The level of care expected in a role — higher for professionals because more skill and knowledge is assumed

Four Elements for Legal Negligence Action

All four must be present for a legal claim to succeed:

1
A duty of care relationship exists
One person's actions could foreseeably affect another's safety or wellbeing. In community services, this relationship always exists between you and your clients.
2
A standard of care applies to the situation
There is a recognised standard for reasonable behaviour. Professional roles have a higher standard — you're expected to have specialist knowledge and skill.
3
The duty of care is breached
The standard of care was not met. If you follow your organisation's policies, work within your role, and apply common sense, you are very unlikely to breach your duty of care.
4
Harm results from the breach
Harm may be physical, psychological, economic, or reputational. If no harm results, there are no grounds for legal action — though disciplinary proceedings are still possible.
📖 Case Study — Learner Guide p. 23

Martin and the Armed Robbery

Martin is a support worker at the bank with his client, Maria. An armed robber bursts in, orders everyone to the floor, and threatens them with a gun. Martin tells Maria to comply and lies next to her, talking quietly to keep her calm.

Martin is not expected to tackle the robber. His duty of care doesn't require him to predict or prevent this extreme, unusual event. He responds as any reasonable person would — protecting Maria within the reality of the situation.

Privacy, Confidentiality & Mandatory Reporting

10:45–11:30 · Learner Guide pp. 25–30

🔒 Section 2.1 · Privacy & Reporting

When do you share information — and when does keeping silent become dangerous?

Confidentiality protects people. So does disclosure, in the right circumstances. Knowing when is the skill.

In crisis work, maintaining privacy is especially important — disclosing personal information can add to trauma and even put clients at greater risk. Imagine sharing a client's address when they're hiding from a violent partner.

🔒

Default Rule

Do not disclose confidential information without your client's consent.

🚨

Exception 1 — Harm

You may disclose if necessary to protect someone from serious harm.

⚖️

Exception 2 — Court

Disclose when formally required by a court of law.

📋

Check Policies

Always check your organisation's confidentiality procedures when safety is at stake.

Mandatory Reporting

In some situations — particularly where a child's safety is at risk — you may be legally required to report regardless of confidentiality. The rules vary by state, territory, and occupation.

📌 When does mandatory reporting apply? ▼
In Victoria, the Children, Youth and Families Act 2005 sets out who is a mandated reporter and what must be reported. Your organisation's policies will specify their requirements. When in doubt — consult your supervisor.
🏛️ Key responsibilities for mandated reporters ▼
Follow legislative procedures for your state/territory. Report concerns to the relevant child protection authority. Document your concerns accurately and objectively. Consult your supervisor if you're unsure whether the threshold is met.
💬 What if the client asks me not to report? ▼
Mandatory reporting obligations exist regardless of client consent. Be transparent with the client about what you're required to do and why — while still fulfilling your legal duty. This is why early conversations about reporting obligations are good practice.
📖 Case Study — Learner Guide p. 30

Elinor and Susie

Elinor is a support worker in an after-school program. Susie, aged seven, frequently arrives in soiled clothing, without shoes, always hungry, appearing underweight and anxious.

One day Susie arrives with a large purple bruise on her face and is limping. When asked what happened, she says: "daddy was very angry with me last night."

Elinor is a mandated reporter. She is bound by legislation to report her concerns about Susie's welfare to the state child protection authority — regardless of what the family might want.

Work Role Boundaries & Organisational Policies

11:30–12:00 · Learner Guide pp. 29–31

📋 Section 2.1 · Work Role Boundaries

Good intentions can still cause harm — why do boundaries exist even when you care deeply?

Working outside your role doesn't just risk breaking rules — it can genuinely hurt the people you're trying to help.

Working outside the boundaries of your job role puts both you and your client at risk. You may cause harm by undertaking tasks you're not qualified for — and expose yourself to a duty of care breach.

1

Always work within your job role description and duty statement

2

Seek advice and supervision when you're unsure of your boundaries

3

Never undertake tasks you're not qualified or competent to perform

4

Refer to your organisation's policies as your first point of reference

5

Document situations where boundary decisions feel difficult

📝

Policy

A statement of intent — what the organisation will do and why. Sets values and direction.

🔢

Procedure

Step-by-step instructions for implementing a policy. The specific actions you take.

📖 Case Study — Learner Guide p. 29

Simon and Martha

Simon is a support worker in a recreational disability program. He's close with many families — including Martha's, a 10-year-old with a learning disability. When Martha's mother needs urgent surgery, Simon offers to have Martha stay at his own home and buys her new clothes from his own pocket.

Simon genuinely cares. But he has stepped well outside his role — creating professional boundary, safeguarding, and liability issues. Even good intentions can lead to serious harm.

Personal Values, Beliefs & Assumptions

12:45–1:30 · Learner Guide pp. 32–34

💬 Section 2.2 · Values & Beliefs

You can't leave yourself at the door — so what do you bring in?

Self-awareness isn't a nice-to-have in community services. It's essential professional infrastructure.

Our personal values and beliefs shape how we see the world — including how we see and respond to clients in crisis. Most of this happens unconsciously, learned through socialisation: absorbing the norms, beliefs, and expectations of our families, communities, and cultures as we grow up.

→
Values: beliefs we hold about what's right and good
E.g. "People should take responsibility for their own problems." A value itself isn't right or wrong — but it shapes the assumptions that follow from it.
→
Assumptions: beliefs we apply to people without evidence
E.g. "People who can't cope in a crisis must be weak." Assumptions take a value and project it onto others — often without us realising we're doing it.
→
Stereotypes: fixed ideas about entire groups of people
E.g. "People with mental illness are dangerous." Stereotypes treat everyone in a group as identical — nearly always negative, and leading to unfair treatment.

🔗 A Common Myth Worth Busting

You might assume only "weak" people experience crisis, or that resilient people just push through. The evidence doesn't support this. Anyone can be overwhelmed. Crisis is a response to a situation exceeding current resources — not a measure of character. Wealth, status, and strength offer no immunity.

Branching Scenario — Priya's Day

2:30–3:00 · Applying today's legal & ethical content

✨ NEW CHARACTER

Meet Priya — Support Worker, Community Mental Health Drop-In

Priya is enthusiastic and genuinely cares about the people she works with. Today she faces real challenges that test her understanding of legal and ethical practice. Help her make the right calls.

Scene 1: Marcus, the adult son of client Wendy (who accesses the service for depression), arrives at the front desk. He asks: "Can you tell me what mum's been talking about in her sessions? I just want to help." Wendy has not authorised anyone to receive information about her care.

What should Priya do?

✅ Exactly right. Priya protects Wendy's confidentiality while showing care for Marcus. She explains the reason and keeps the door open. Empathetic, ethical, and legally sound.
❌ Not quite. Even with good intentions, sharing Wendy's information without consent is a confidentiality breach — and potentially illegal. Family concern doesn't override client rights.
🟡 Partially right. Not sharing is correct — but dismissing Marcus without explanation is cold and unhelpful. A brief, empathetic response that honours both client rights and his concern is the better approach.
Scene 2: Later, Priya visits Daniel, a client with schizophrenia who receives regular home support. He comments: "I've been thinking it would be better if I wasn't here anymore." He quickly changes the subject. Priya is a support worker — not a clinician.

What does Priya do first?

✅ Correct. Asking directly about suicidality reduces risk — it shows you care and opens the door for Daniel to share. Listen, stay calm, then contact your supervisor for guidance.
❌ This risks missing a crisis. Indirect statements about not wanting to be here are warning signs that warrant a direct, compassionate follow-up. Ignoring them to "avoid upsetting" a client can leave someone in danger.
🟡 Good instinct, wrong order. Stay with Daniel and ask directly first — then call your supervisor. Don't leave him alone with that comment hanging.
Scene 3: Daniel confirms he's been having regular thoughts of suicide and hasn't taken his medication for two weeks. A CATT referral is being arranged. Priya stays with Daniel. He asks: "You won't tell anyone else about this, will you? I don't want to end up in hospital."

What does Priya say?

✅ Honest, warm, and ethical. Priya acknowledges Daniel's fear, is transparent about what's happening, and reassures him she's staying. Promising false confidentiality would harm trust and breach duty of care.
❌ Promising false confidentiality is a serious problem. When Daniel finds out, trust is permanently destroyed. In a safety situation, transparency delivered with kindness is always the right call.
🟡 Avoidance isn't the answer. Daniel has asked a direct question. Deflecting leaves him anxious and uncertain. A kind, honest answer — even a difficult one — respects his dignity.
Scene 4: The CATT team has arrived and Daniel is safely transferred. After they leave, Priya's supervisor offers a debrief: "How are you going?" Priya feels shaken but her instinct is to say she's fine and get back to work quickly.

What's the most professionally sound response?

✅ This is what good self-care looks like in practice. Accepting the debrief, being honest about emotional impact, and staying connected to the professional process are all part of ethical practice. Self-care isn't just rest — it's using your organisation's support structures.
❌ Suppressing your emotional response to critical incidents leads to vicarious trauma and burnout. Your organisation's debrief process exists precisely for moments like this. Seeking support is a professional obligation, not a weakness.
🟡 Rest is important — but disappearing without the debrief leaves key steps undone. Debrief with your supervisor first, then rest if needed.
🏆

Scenario Complete — Priya navigated it beautifully.

She protected confidentiality, responded to suicidality safely, was honest under pressure, and accepted debriefing support. That's ethical practice in action.
