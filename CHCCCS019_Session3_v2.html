Stages of Crisis & Intervention Principles

10:15am – 10:30am · Learner Guide p.39–41

📖 Section 3.1 · p.39

When crisis hits, what do you do — and in what order?

Crisis intervention follows stages. Knowing the sequence helps you stay anchored when everything feels chaotic.

People experiencing crisis move through stages, which means your response needs to adapt. Click each stage to read more.

1
Recognise there is a crisis
Before you can help, you need to name what's happening. Look for direct and indirect indicators — changes in behaviour, distorted perceptions, inability to function normally. Naming it helps the person feel seen.
2
Identify the significance of the triggering event
What happened? How significant is this event to this person? The same event affects people very differently. Understanding the trigger helps you calibrate your response.
3
Release tension — listen and provide emotional support
Before problem-solving, the person needs to feel heard. Use active listening — reflect feelings, paraphrase, validate. Don't rush to "fix" it. This stage creates safety that allows everything else to happen.
4
Encourage realistic thinking (cognitive restructuring)
In crisis, people often catastrophise. Gently challenge distortions by helping the person focus on realities. What's actually true right now? What's manageable today?
5
Make plans — prioritise needs, set achievable goals
Work with the person to identify what needs to happen first. What's the most urgent thing? Plans should be SMART — specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, timely.
6
Build on existing strengths — empower and offer alternatives
What has this person survived before? What resources do they already have? A strengths-based approach reminds the person that they have capacity, even when it doesn't feel that way.

Principles rapid fire

Crisis intervention is like "first aid for mental health" — it stabilises, not resolves.
✅ True. Crisis intervention = short-term, immediate stabilisation. It's first aid — not therapy. Referral handles what comes next.
Workers should be mostly directive in crisis — the client can't be trusted to make decisions.
❌ False. Empowerment is a core principle. You balance direction with collaboration — you lead when needed, but always work with the person wherever possible.
"Cognitive restructuring" means helping someone replace catastrophic thinking with a more realistic view.
✅ True. Stage 4: encouraging realistic thinking. Not toxic positivity — just gently challenging "my life is over" with "what's actually true right now?"
Responding quickly is a key crisis intervention principle, because timing affects outcomes.
✅ True. "Respond quickly" is one of the 6 common principles. Crisis is time-sensitive — early intervention changes trajectory.

📖 Section 3.1 continued · p.41

Think of crisis intervention as "first aid for mental health"

Practical, non-intrusive, focused on immediate safety and connection.

Psychological First Aid (PFA) provides psychological, social and practical support. Crucially, it doesn't pressure people to talk — it creates safety and connection.

👂Listen — but don't pressure people to talk
🕊️Provide comfort — help people feel calm
🔗Connect to information, services & supports

Critical Incidents, Emergency & Safety

12:45pm – 1:45pm · Learner Guide pp.43–51

⚠️ This section covers critical incidents including self-harm, violence and emergency responses. Take care of yourself as you engage.
📖 Section 3.2 · pp.43–48

What happens when crisis escalates into a genuine emergency?

Critical incidents require a different level of response — clear procedures, organisational support, and debrief.

A critical incident is any sudden, unexpected event that overwhelms a person's coping and poses serious risk to life, health or safety. In community services, this is broader than most people expect.

🚨 What counts as a critical incident? ▾

In general: assault, threats, severe injury, fire, death, bomb threat.

In community services: self-harm, violent behaviours, missing clients, physical & sexual assaults, medication errors, suicide or attempted suicide — and more.

📋 Critical Incident Stress Management (7 steps) ▾
  1. Prepare — education and training before incidents occur
  2. Large-scale strategies — information, networking, community support
  3. Defusing — immediately after the incident
  4. Debriefing — usually within a week
  5. Crisis intervention counselling — individual face-to-face support
  6. Family intervention — if required
  7. Referral — to follow-up services as needed

🔗 Referrals · p.50

Once immediate crisis is contained, you may need to refer your client to other services for follow-up. Referral is not just "sending someone somewhere" — it's a collaborative process that requires consent, information, and follow-through.

🤝Discuss options with the client — get their consent
ℹ️Provide enough information for an informed choice
✅Check eligibility criteria before referring
📋Follow your organisation's referral procedure
📄Collect and share relevant client information
🔄Follow up — check how the referral is working
📖 Case Study · p.50

Amanda & Elena — Referral in Action

Elena has post-natal depression and has refused counselling and support groups. Amanda listens carefully to Elena's fears (rooted in her grandmother's hospitalisation), asks what would make things different, presents a range of options, lets Elena choose, and offers to accompany her to the first appointment.

🆕 Introducing Leilani
Scene 1 of 4

The home visit — something isn't right

You're Leilani, a support worker at a community mental health service. You've arrived for a scheduled visit with Marcus, 34, who has been managing well for months. Today feels different — he's pacing, won't make eye contact, arms covered despite warm weather. He says "fine, you can go."

What do you do?

✅ Right call. You've spotted multiple indirect indicators — pacing, avoidance, covered arms, flat affect. Acknowledging what you observe without diagnosing is crisis-aware practice at its best. This is stage 1 (recognise) and stage 3 (release tension through presence).
❌ Not yet. "Fine" in crisis often means the opposite. Marcus is showing multiple indirect indicators — and people in early crisis often push helpers away. Leaving without acknowledging what you've observed misses a critical safety signal.
🟡 Close. Consulting your supervisor is right — but not before attempting to engage. Calling from the doorstep signals to Marcus that you're not really present. Stay, engage, then consult.
Scene 2 of 4

Marcus discloses — now what?

Marcus pulls up his sleeves and shows you fresh cuts on his forearms. He says he's been cutting himself for a week when he feels overwhelmed. He looks ashamed and adds: "I'm not trying to kill myself — it's the only thing that makes the feelings stop."

What do you do first?

Scene 3 of 4

Assessing risk — asking the hard question

You've listened to Marcus for 15 minutes. He's calmer but flat. You know self-harm and suicidal ideation can co-exist, even when the person distinguishes them. You need to ask directly. How?

Scene 4 of 4

Planning next steps — collaboration in action

Marcus says he is not suicidal. He's been isolated and overwhelmed since losing his job three weeks ago. He's scared of being hospitalised, so hasn't told his psychiatrist. You need to help him plan next steps. How do you approach this?

🏆

Scenario complete — well done, Leilani.

You stayed present, asked the hard questions directly, and worked collaboratively with Marcus while honouring your duty of care. That's crisis intervention done well.
